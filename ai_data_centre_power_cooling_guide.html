CTO guide · AI-ready data centre infrastructure

Power and cooling now define the AI platform.

AI infrastructure design has moved from “how many GPUs?” to “how many sustainable kilowatts per rack can the site support, remove, monitor and grow?” This guide gives a practical framework for sizing IT load, translating it to grid power, understanding PUE, and choosing the right cooling architecture.

PUE
Total facility energy ÷ IT equipment energy
kW/rack
The design constraint for AI density, not just floor space
DLC
Direct liquid cooling for high-density GPU and accelerator heat
What matters most

Start with power, then decide how to remove heat.

1. Size the IT load

Build the demand model from servers, GPUs, storage, networking, management nodes and realistic utilisation. For AI clusters, GPU thermal design power and concurrency drive the rack plan.

2. Convert IT load to grid load

Grid power is not the same as IT power. Grid power must include cooling, UPS losses, electrical distribution, pumps, lighting and other facility overheads through PUE.

3. Select the cooling envelope

Air remains valid at lower rack densities. Hybrid and rear-door heat exchange extend existing sites. Direct liquid cooling and immersion support the highest AI densities.

Cooling decision path

Enhanced air

Use where density is moderate, whitespace is available, and the site can manage hot/cold aisle containment and airflow discipline.

Rear-door heat exchange

Use to capture heat at rack exhaust, reduce room heat load, and add density without redesigning every server for liquid.

Direct liquid cooling

Use when GPU density, sustained accelerator TDP, noise, fan power or PUE targets make air alone inefficient or impractical.

Immersion

Use for specialised, ultra-dense environments where operational model, serviceability, fluids, warranties and facilities are designed around immersion from day one.

Power calculation

Translate IT power into facility and grid power.

For executive planning, separate three values: IT power, facility overhead, and grid power. IT power is what the compute, storage and network consume. Grid power is what the utility must deliver to run the whole data centre.

Total facility / grid power
=
IT power × PUE
Facility overhead
=
IT power × (PUE − 1)

When liquid cooling becomes the strategic choice

Technical triggers

High accelerator TDP, high kW per rack, sustained training or inferencing, fan power escalation, hot spots, thermal throttling, or inability to add enough air-cooled racks.

Business triggers

Grid constraints, high energy cost, limited whitespace, faster time-to-capacity, sustainability targets, noise reduction, and a need for repeatable rack-scale deployment.

Liquid cooling architecture

Primary and secondary loops keep facility water away from IT coolant.

Primary loop

Facility water system connected to chillers, dry coolers, cooling towers or other heat rejection equipment. It feeds the CDU or rear-door heat exchanger.

→

CDU / heat exchanger

Separates the facility side from the IT side, controls temperature, flow and pressure, and transfers heat between loops without mixing fluids.

→

Secondary loop

Conditioned coolant network serving rack manifolds, quick disconnects and cold plates mounted to CPUs or GPUs.

What must be engineered for DLC

Facility prerequisites

Water availability, heat rejection capacity, approach temperature, filtration, floor loading, pipe routes, leak detection, humidity control and maintenance access.

IT prerequisites

DLC-ready servers, rack manifolds, CDUs, supported coolant, quick disconnects, service procedures, telemetry, and operational runbooks.

Operations prerequisites

Water chemistry plan, coolant testing, spares, trained facilities and IT teams, change control for adding/removing nodes, and incident response for leaks or thermal events.

Risk controls

Do not mix unsupported devices or fluids on the same secondary network. Keep primary and secondary loop responsibilities clear across facilities, IT and vendors.
